Local Elements

Look for one of these amazing endangered or threated species in your Brazil WonderStamp. We hope this project helps to bring light to the conservation efforts protecting them.

Rock Cavy

"Rock cavies are endemic to several protected areas, and reproduce quickly, so they are currently listed as "least concern" on the IUCN Red List. They are frequently hunted as food by local human populations, which resulted in population decline of 30% in the last 10 years. Their range is also being reduced due to deforestation. Efforts are underway to breed rock cavies in captivity as a potential food source."

Source:

Giant Anteater

"The giant anteater is listed as vulnerable by the International Union for Conservation of Nature. It has been extirpated from many parts of its former range. Threats to its survival include habitat destruction, fire, and poaching for fur and bushmeat, although some anteaters inhabit protected areas."

Source:

Maned Wolf

"This mammal lives in open and semi-open habitats, especially grasslands with scattered bushes and trees, in the Cerrado of south, central-west, and southeastern Brazil; Paraguay; northern Argentina; and Bolivia east and north of the Andes, and far southeastern Peru. The International Union for Conservation of Nature lists it as near threatened, while it is considered a vulnerable species by the Brazilian Institute of Environment and Renewable Natural Resources."

Source:

Southern Right Whale

"The southern right whale, listed as "endangered" by CITES, is protected by all countries with known breeding populations (Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Chile, New Zealand, South Africa and Uruguay). In Brazil, a federal Environmental Protection Area encompassing some 1,560 km2 (600 sq mi) and 130 km (81 mi) of coastline in Santa Catarina State was established in 2000 to protect the species' main breeding grounds in Brazil and promote regulated whale watching."

Source:

Giant Armadillo

"The giant armadillo is the largest living species of armadillo. It lives in South America, ranging throughout as far south as northern Argentina. This species is considered vulnerable to extinction."

Source:

Brazilian Tapir

"The South American tapir, also commonly called the Brazilian tapir is one of the four widely recognized species in the tapir family. It is the largest surviving native terrestrial mammal in the Amazon. The dwindling numbers of the South American tapir are due to poaching for meat and hide, as well as habitat destruction."

Source:

Golden Bellied Capuchin

"Populations of the Golden Bellied Capuchin are restricted to the Atlantic forest of south-eastern Bahia, Brazil, due possibly to high degrees of interference from humans. Historically they probably would have inhabited the entire area east of, and north to, the Rio SÃ£o Francisco. The largest continuous area of forest in its known range, the Una Biological Reserve in Bahia, is estimated to contain a population of 185 individuals. As of 2004, there were 85 individuals in zoos and breeding facilities in Europe and Brazil."

Source:

Spectacled Owl

"The spectacled owl occurs over a very large range and is still a resident in much of its range. Due to this, it is classified as Least Concern by the IUCN. However, being a large, slow-maturing bird of prey with a strong sense of territoriality, it as a rule occurs at low densities. In areas where prey populations are hunted by people and habitats are destroyed or compromised, they may decrease."

Source:
